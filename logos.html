Editframe — Round 3

Back from the woods. No more grids of boxes. Time is not a rectangle. Video is not a database. Let's find what this thing actually wants to look like.
I. The Splice
A cut isn't a button you press. It's the moment one reality ends and another begins. What does that look like as a mark?
R3-01 — Lightning Splice
A jagged cut tears through the wordmark at the edit point. "edit" is bright — present tense. "frame" is dim — already past. The red lightning bolt is the knife. The cut is the product. Every time you read the name you perform the edit.
R3-02 — ef Ligature / Shared Bar
The crossbar of the f and the midline of the e are the same stroke — a single horizontal blade that cuts through both letterforms simultaneously. The ligature is the edit. The two letters can't be separated from the cut that joins them.
R3-03 — Film Gate / Light Leak
A film frame held up to light — nearly black, with a single crack of white cutting through. The light is what the camera captures. The darkness is the gate. The crack is where editing happens — the seam between two realities. Extremely minimal. The wordmark in thin-weight spaced capitals.
R3-04 — Two Clocks, One Hand
Two timelines overlap. They share an edit point — a single hand that belongs to both. Source clip time and composition time are different. The red pivot is where they meet. Deceptively simple. Also reads as a Venn with a clock — the intersection of two temporal contexts.
II. The Frame as Phenomenology
Not a film frame. Not a code bracket. The frame as the philosophical act of selection — you frame something by deciding what's inside and what's outside.
R3-05 — The Unfinished Frame
Three corners solid, one ghosted — the frame is still being composed. The missing corner is the product's invitation: you complete it. It's also just visually interesting — the asymmetry creates tension and motion. The eye travels to the missing corner. That's where your content goes.
R3-06 — Infinite Regress / Zoom
Four nested corner-bracket frames, each one brighter, converging on a red point. The zoom-in as the fundamental editing gesture. Also: compositions inside compositions, timegroups inside timegroups. The red center is where you are right now. There's always another frame inside.
R3-07 — The Loop / Infinite Timeline
A figure-eight that passes through itself — the looping composition, the render that feeds back into itself. Time that has no end, only a current position (the red dot). The crossing point is where past and future share a frame. More mathematical than practical — but isn't that what render pipelines are?
R3-08 — The Frame as Coordinate
An infinite grid of dormant points — one square blazes red. Frame 47 of 240. The render cursor. A pixel is just a location — it has no meaning until it's illuminated. The mark is the act of selection: saying "this frame, right here." Sparse. Contemplative. Unusual for a tech logo.
III. Time as Material
What if time had texture? Weight? What if you could hold a second in your hands and it had grain, thickness, edge?
R3-09 — Geological Strata
Time as sediment — each layer a track, each stratum a moment compressed into material. The amber layer is where you're editing right now. Video archaeology. Technically unrelated to the product, spiritually accurate about what a timeline is: a cross-section through accumulated time. An unusual, memorable mark.
R3-10 — Waveform EF
The initials "EF" filled with a waveform — the audio fingerprint of the edit. The letterforms are carved out of time-data rather than drawn. One bar in the F is red — the playhead crossing the waveform at this exact moment. Technical, dense, reveals itself slowly.
R3-11 — The Ribbon / Evolution
A single strip that transforms as it folds — film strip → timeline → frame. The history of video editing in one continuous material. The final face-on rectangle is what Editframe produces: a frame, rendered. The red tick mark is the playhead mid-journey. More illustrative than mark — but the idea of transformation is the product.
R3-12 — The Dissolve
The transition itself as a mark — white to red to black, the cross-dissolve frozen at the 50% point. "edit" almost invisible on the white side; "frame" almost invisible on the black side. The brand lives in the moment of maximum ambiguity — the instant that is both and neither. Not a scalable mark but a brand declaration.
IV. Signals in the Noise
Code is signal. Distributed systems are noise becoming signal. What marks feel like finding the right frequency?
R3-13 — Frame Barcode
Every frame in a video has a unique signature. This is a barcode of frames — each bar a timestamp, the red bar the current edit point. Also: barcodes are machine-readable. Editframe is an API — it's meant to be read by machines. The wordmark spaced beneath reads like a product number. Clean on white.
R3-14 — Signal Pulse
Silence — then a single square-wave event — then silence. The render job: nothing, then everything, then done. The red line bisects the pulse at its axis — the edit point. Square wave rather than sine because renders are not continuous; they're discrete events in time. The flat lines are emptiness waiting.
R3-15 — Recursive Name Tree
The product name split recursively into a binary tree — editframe → edit + frame → ed + it + fra + me → e + d + i + t… A composition is its own name decomposed. Every render is a parse tree. Typography as data structure. Works only at large sizes — this is a concept piece, not a scalable mark.
R3-16 — The Missing Hand
A clock with only one hand — the minute hand says where you are; the hour hand is gone, replaced by a red dot. In video time, you know where you are in the clip but not where you are in the day. The red dot is the edit cursor, floating free of the clock mechanism. A clock that measures something other than clock time.
V. Pure Mark Experiments
Forget what it "means." What shape wants to exist? What form is inevitable?
R3-17 — Double Slash
// is the comment. The aside. The cut that removes. In film, a diagonal slash marks a take to reject. In URLs, it follows the protocol. Two slashes is the typographic gesture of both "dismissed" and "begin." Bold, fast, directional. Extremely recognizable to developers. Zero ambiguity about audience.
R3-18 — The Pipeline (Pipes)
Three pipes | | | — each is a stage in the render pipeline, each slightly lighter than the last, data flowing through. The | is also the cursor. Also the Unix pipe operator: `input | process | output`. Three stages: ingest, compose, render. Bold on white. Extremely simple. Possibly too simple — or exactly right.
R3-19 — Hourglass / Render Funnel
Source material (white, full) flowing through the pinch point (red — the edit) into the render output (dark, compressed). The hourglass is the entire pipeline: everything you have becomes one frame at a time. The pinch is where Editframe lives — between raw material and finished output.
R3-20 — Aperture / Iris
A camera iris — six blades converging on a bright center. The aperture is literally how a frame is opened and closed. The white hexagon is the gate — the frame itself. The red center is the sensor, the destination. Risk: camera/photography brands own this form. But the geometric abstraction is distinct — these are sharp rectangles, not organic blades.
R3-21 — Single-Stroke EF
Both letterforms drawn as a single unbroken path — the edit is one continuous action from start to finish. The stroke passes through both letters without lifting, the crossbar serving both e and f simultaneously. Handcrafted feeling despite geometric precision. More human than system, which may be exactly right for a tool that serves human creativity.
R3-22 — Playhead Entering the Frame
A bracket (the frame, the API context) and a playhead (time, the edit cursor) in collision. The moment the playhead enters the frame is where Editframe operates. The red intersection dot is the product: the point where time meets structure. Two of the strongest shapes from earlier rounds, finally in direct relationship.
